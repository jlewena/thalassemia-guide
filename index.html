Understanding Beta Thalassemia

A plain-language guide to what this condition is, how it affects the body, and how it is passed from parent to child.

1

Your blood and red blood cells

Your blood is made up of billions of tiny cells. The most important for carrying oxygen around your body are red blood cells. Every organ in your body depends on red blood cells to deliver the oxygen it needs to function.

Think of it this way Red blood cells are like delivery trucks. They pick up oxygen from your lungs and drop it off to every part of your body, then return to collect more.

Inside each red blood cell is a protein called haemoglobin. Haemoglobin is what actually grabs and holds the oxygen. A healthy haemoglobin molecule is made of four protein chains. Two of these are called alpha chains and two called beta chains. These are held together like a four-piece puzzle.

How a healthy haemoglobin molecule is built

Both alpha and beta chains must be present in equal amounts for haemoglobin to work properly. Beta thalassemia specifically affects the beta chains.

2

Genes and the HBB gene

Your body builds everything it needs by following instructions stored in your DNA. This includes haemoglobin. These instructions are divided into sections called genes. You have around 20,000 genes in total, and each one carries the recipe for a different protein.

The gene responsible for building the beta chain of haemoglobin is called the HBB gene. You inherit two copies of every gene — one from your mother and one from your father.

Key point A gene mutation is a small change in the DNA instructions. You can think of this like a typo in a recipe. Depending on where the typo is, it might produce a slightly different result, or no result at all.
What does a gene mutation actually mean?

Your DNA is made up of a long sequence of four main chemicals. These are often represented by letters: A, T, C, and G. A gene mutation means that one or more of these letters has changed, is missing, or has been duplicated. In beta thalassemia, there are over 300 known mutations in the HBB gene. Most cause the cell to produce either too little beta chain protein, or none at all.

Why do I have two copies of each gene?

Humans inherit one set of chromosomes, and therefore one copy of each gene from each parent. Having two copies provides a natural backup. In many conditions, if one copy is faulty, the other working copy is enough to produce a sufficient amount of the protein. This is the case in beta thalassemia - one working HBB gene can produce enough beta chain on its own, which is why carriers do not develop the disease. The version of the disease where both copies are faulty causes the most issues and is termed thalassemia major.

3

What goes wrong in beta thalassemia

Beta thalassemia occurs when both copies of the HBB gene are mutated. With faulty instructions in both copies, the body can't produce enough, or sometimes not produce any functional beta chains.

This triggers a chain reaction inside the bone marrow, where red blood cells are made:

1

Too few beta chains are produced

The mutated HBB gene produces little or no beta chain protein, while alpha chain production continues normally.

2

Alpha chains build up and become toxic

Without beta chains to pair with, excess alpha chains clump together inside developing red blood cells. These clumps are toxic and damage the cell from the inside.

3

Red blood cell production fails

Most damaged red blood cells die inside the bone marrow before they are ever released into the bloodstream. This is called ineffective erythropoiesis, where the bone marrow is working very hard but producing very few working cells.

4

The spleen and liver destroy the surviving cells

The few red blood cells that do make it into the bloodstream are fragile and misshapen. The spleen and liver recognise them as damaged and remove them early. This creates a cyclical effect, further reducing the number of red blood cells in circulation.

5

The result: chronic severe anaemia

With far too few working red blood cells, the body can't deliver enough oxygen. This is the central problem in beta thalassemia major.

The body tries to compensate When the bone marrow fails to produce enough red blood cells, the body desperately expands it. This can cause bones to widen and change shape. The spleen and liver also try to take over red blood cell production, growing very large in the process. These compensatory changes create their own serious complications.
What happens with iron?

Haemoglobin normally contains iron at its core. When red blood cells are destroyed in large numbers, all of that iron is released. The body has no good way to get rid of excess iron, so it accumulates in the heart, liver, and hormone-producing glands, thus causing damage to these organs over time. The body also absorbs more iron from food than normal, because a faulty signalling molecule (called hepcidin) is suppressed, further worsening the buildup.

4

Why symptoms happen

Every symptom of beta thalassemia major connects directly to the cellular problems described above. In other words, the symptoms are the consequences which follow from your body having too few working red blood cells.

😮‍💨

Severe tiredness and breathlessness

Fewer red blood cells means less oxygen reaches the muscles and brain. The body can't sustain normal activity.

🦴

Bone changes and facial features

The bone marrow expands in an attempt to produce more red blood cells, pushing outward and changing the shape of bones, particularly in the face and skull.

🫀

Enlarged spleen and liver

These organs take on the job of producing red blood cells when the bone marrow can't keep up, causing them to enlarge significantly.

💛

Jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes)

When large numbers of red blood cells are destroyed, a yellow pigmented waste product called bilirubin is released faster than the liver can clear it.

🫀

Heart and organ damage

Iron that accumulates from destroyed red blood cells deposits in the heart, liver, and hormone glands, causing progressive damage to these organs.

📉

Slow growth in children

Without enough oxygen being delivered to tissues, normal growth and development is impaired from early childhood.

Severity varies Beta thalassemia exists on a spectrum. People with one faulty HBB gene copy (carriers) have no symptoms. Those with two faulty copies may have mild disease (thalassemia intermedia) or severe disease (thalassemia major), depending on the specific mutations involved.
5

How beta thalassemia is inherited

Beta thalassemia follows a pattern called autosomal recessive inheritance. In plain terms, this means a child needs to inherit a faulty HBB gene copy from both parents to develop the disease. Inheriting just one faulty copy makes a person a carrier — healthy, but able to pass the gene on.

Being a carrier Carriers have one working HBB gene and one faulty one. The working copy produces enough beta chain on its own, so carriers do not develop thalassemia and are typically in full health.

These probabilities apply independently to every pregnancy. Having one child with a particular outcome does not change the odds for the next pregnancy.

Why are these probabilities equal each time?

Each parent passes on exactly one of their two HBB gene copies to each child. This is chosen at random during the formation of egg and sperm cells. Because this is random every time, the outcome of a previous pregnancy has no influence on the next. It is similar to flipping a coin: getting heads once does not make heads less likely on the next flip.

What if only one parent is a carrier?

If only one parent carries a faulty HBB gene copy, none of their children can inherit two faulty copies, meaning none can develop beta thalassemia major. Each child would have a 50% chance of being a carrier and a 50% chance of inheriting no faulty copies at all. This is why partner testing is important after a carrier result.
